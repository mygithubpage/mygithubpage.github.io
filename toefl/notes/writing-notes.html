Core Concept: Writing

PART 1: EXPRESSING YOUR PURPOSE

Most writing tasks on the TOEFL ask you to do one of the following: Present your opinion on an issue (a value essay, Independent). Evaluate the information on a topic (a fact essay, Integrated).

In general, your introduction needs to accomplish the following tasks:

  1. Introduce the topic of your discussion.
  2. Present your thesis statement.
Writing an Effective Introduction Paragraph

Step 1: State Your Position

It's best to make a clear, direct statement of your position. Try using some of the following phrases: I believe, I feel that, In my opinion, In my view, I think that, I do not believe, I do not feel that, My position is, It is my belief that.

Step 2: State the Reason for Your Opinion

When stating why you believe your position, it may be helpful to use some of the following phrases: I believe this because, I feel this way because, The reason I think this is, This is because, Since

PART 2: ORGANIZING YOUR IDEAS

Writing an Effective Body Paragraph

Step 1: The Topic Sentence

A topic sentence should introduce the main point of the paragraph. To make a clear transition statement, use the topic or thesis of the previous paragraph as a starting point. repeats an idea found in the last sentence of the introduction. Using this technique makes your writing flow better and appear more organized.

Step 2: The Examples

your model is still the same. state the example or reason (what the example is), explain its significance (why the example is important)

Supporting Examples: They are used to show the reader why you have a particular viewpoint or position.

Summarizing Examples: Some tasks require you to explain or summarize someone else's opinion. For these tasks, you are not trying to convince the reader of anything; instead, your purpose is to report what you've read or heard.

The Example Template

When you use an example in an essay, try to follow this template.

  1. Sentence 1: Introduce the example and tie it back to your thesis.
  2. Sentence 2: State one reason why your example is important or relevant to your thesis.
  3. Sentence 3: Add detail to reason #1.
  4. Sentence 4: State another reason why your example is important or relevant to your thesis.
  5. Sentence 5: Add detail to reason #2.
  6. Sentence 6 (optional): Provide a summary of your reasons, and relate them back to your thesis.

PART 3: CONCLUDING YOUR RESPONSE

A conclusion has to be only two or three sentences long. After you restate your thesis, repeat your reasons or make a final statement about the correctness of your views.

Cracking the Writing Section

CRACKING THE WRITING SECTION: BASIC PRINCIPLES

When writing your essay, be aware of the following important points:

  • Make sure sure you answer the question appropriately.
  • Make sure sure your essay is long enough.
  • Make sure sure your essay is clearly organized.

CRACKING THE WRITING SECTION: BASIC APPROACH

Template #1: Casting Doubt on a Lecture

Summarize the points made in the lecture, explaining how they cast doubt on the reading.

  1. Paragraph #1: Introduction

    1. Topic sentence: In the lecture, the (professor/teacher/instructor) made several points about (topic).
    2. State main idea of lecture: The (professor/teacher/instructor) argues that (the main idea of the lecture).
    3. Transition/main idea of reading: However, the reading contends that (the main idea of the reading).
    4. Thesis statement: The professor's lecture casts doubt on the reading by using a number of points that are contrary to (the main idea of the reading).

  2. Paragraph #2: Body Paragraph

    1. Transition/point #1 from lecture: The first point that the (professor/teacher/instructor) uses to cast doubt on the reading is (point #1 from lecture).
    2. Detail for point #1: According to the (professor/teacher/instructor), (detail for point #1 from the lecture).
    3. Opposing point from reading: (Point #1) differs from the reading in that the reading states (point #1 of the reading).
    4. Explanation of relationship between reading and lecture: The point made by the (professor/lecturer/instructor) casts doubt on the reading because (how lecture is different from reading).

  3. Paragraph #3: Body Paragraph

  4. Paragraph #4: Body Paragraph

  5. Paragraph #4: Conclusion

    1. Topic sentence In conclusion, the points made in the lecture contrast with the reading.
    2. Summary (Points from the lecture) demonstrate that (main idea of the reading) is in doubt.
Template #2: Using Specific Details and Examples to Support Your Opinion

Do you agree or disagree with the following statement? (statement) Use specific details and examples to support your answer.

  1. Paragraph #1: Introduction

    1. Topic sentence: The issue at hand is (choice offered by the prompt).
    2. Interpret the prompt: This issue is (important/difficult/troubling) because (what is important/difficult/troubling about the prompt).
    3. State your thesis: I believe that (state your choice) is the better option because (reasons why you believe your option is preferable).

  2. Paragraph #2: Body Paragraph

    1. Transition/first reason: (Your choice of options) is preferable because (reason #1).
    2. Detail for point #1: (Details about reason #1).
    3. Tie reason #1 back to thesis: Because (details about reason #1), I think that (your choice) is superior to (the other option).

  3. Paragraph #3: Body Paragraph

  4. Paragraph #4: Body Paragraph

  5. Paragraph #4: Conclusion

    1. Transition/restate thesis: Ultimately, I feel that (your choice) is the correct one.
    2. Final statement: I believe this because (why you believe your choice is best).

Integrated Writing

Strategies for Raising Your Score on the Integrated Writing Task

As you read:

  • The main idea often has to do with some policy or practice or some position on an issue. Or it may have to do with proposing some overall hypothesis about the way some process or procedure works.
  • See how this main idea is evaluated or developed: Arguments or explanations are presented that support/doubt the main position. Policy or practice will (not) be beneficial or prove useful or advisable.

As you listen:

Listen for information, examples, or explanations that make points in the reading passage seem wrong or less convincing or even untrue

As you write your response:

Your response should contain the following:

  1. The specific ideas, explanations, and arguments in the lecture that oppose or challenge points in the reading.
  2. Coherent and accurate presentations of each point that you make; that is, the language you use should make sense and should accurately reflect the ideas presented in the lecture and the reading.
  3. A clear, coherent structure that enables the reader to understand what points in the lecture relate to what points in the reading.
  • The main point is in the end or middle of the first paragraph, rarely in the beginning of it.
  • Sub point is the first or last sentence of the supporting paragraph.
  • Using Reading passage sub point details to focus on the listening passage.
  • Try to write down as many detail keywords in the listening passage as possible.
  • Noticed the Proper Nouns in the Reading Passage.

Independent Writing

Development is the amount and kinds of support (details, examples, reasons) for your ideas that you present in your essay. The raters will judge whether you have addressed the topic and how well your details, examples, reasons support your ideas.

Organization is something that raters notice-when you fail to organize. Writing in paragraphs and marking transitions from one idea to another in various ways usually helps the reader to follow your ideas. But be aware that just using transition words such as first or second does not guarantee that your essay is organized. The points you make must all relate to the topic of the essay and to the main idea you are presenting in response. To earn a top score, you need to avoid redundancy (repetition of ideas), digression (points that are not related to your main point, that take away from the "unity" of your ideas), and unclear connections (places where it is hard for the reader to understand how two ideas or parts of your writing are related).

Language use is the third criterion on which your essay will be judged. There should be a variety of sentence structures, and word choice should be appropriate. If your essay includes a few minor lexical or grammar errors, you can still get a high score.
